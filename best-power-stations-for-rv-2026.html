Best Portable Power Stations for RVs in 2026

Updated March 2026 · 14 min read

RV life demands reliable power. Whether you are running a fridge, charging devices, powering a CPAP machine at night, or keeping the lights on during a boondocking trip, a portable power station is one of the smartest upgrades you can make to your rig. Unlike a noisy gas generator, a modern power station runs silently, produces zero fumes, and can recharge from solar panels while you drive or park in the sun.

We evaluated over 30 portable power stations specifically for RV use, focusing on the things that matter most on the road: high capacity for extended off-grid stays, sufficient output to handle RV appliances, manageable weight, solar charging capability, and reliable build quality that holds up to vibration and temperature swings.

1. EcoFlow Delta Pro 3 — Best for Full-Time RV Living

If you live in your RV full-time or take extended boondocking trips, the EcoFlow Delta Pro 3 is the power station that can keep up with a household-level energy demand. With 4,096 watt-hours of LiFePO4 battery capacity and 4,000 watts of continuous AC output, it can run an RV air conditioner, a full-size residential fridge, a microwave, and still have power left over for your devices.

The Delta Pro 3 also supports expansion batteries, so you can scale up to a staggering 48 kWh if needed. For RV owners who wire it into their electrical panel, it functions as a seamless backup system that kicks in automatically when shore power drops. The built-in NEMA 14-50 outlet is designed specifically for RV hookups.

The main downsides are obvious: it weighs 114 pounds and costs between $2,500 and $3,500. But for serious RV travelers who want generator-level power without the generator, nothing else comes close.

2. Bluetti AC200L — Best Value for RV Use

The Bluetti AC200L delivers an incredible amount of power for the price. With 2,048 watt-hours of capacity and 2,400 watts of continuous output, it can handle the vast majority of RV appliances: a 12V fridge, a coffee maker, laptop charging, LED lighting, and fans running simultaneously.

What makes the AC200L particularly well-suited for RV use is its port selection. Four AC outlets, two USB-C ports, two USB-A ports, a 30A RV outlet, two DC outputs, and a wireless charging pad built into the top. The LiFePO4 battery is rated for 3,500+ cycles, meaning it will outlast most RVs.

At 62 pounds, the AC200L is heavy but not unmanageable. Most RV owners keep it in a fixed spot inside the rig and rarely move it, so weight is less of an issue than it would be for camping. Prices frequently dip below $750 on sale, which is outstanding for this much capacity.

How to Choose the Right Power Station for Your RV

Calculate your daily energy needs

List every device and appliance you plan to power from the station. Multiply each device's wattage by the number of hours you expect to run it daily. Add them up for your total daily watt-hour requirement. A typical RV setup running a 12V fridge (50W), phone charging (20W), LED lights (30W), and a laptop (60W) for a full day uses roughly 1,500-2,000 Wh.

Consider your RV's existing electrical system

If your RV already has a robust house battery bank and inverter, a smaller power station as a supplement might be all you need. If you are starting from scratch or your RV's electrical system is basic, a larger unit like the Delta Pro 3 or AC200L can serve as your primary power source.

Solar charging is essential for boondocking

If you spend any time off-grid, solar recharging capability is non-negotiable. Look for power stations with high solar input ratings. Roof-mounted rigid panels on an RV deliver the most consistent output, but portable folding panels work well too and give you flexibility to chase the sun.

Weight and mounting considerations

Heavy power stations (60+ pounds) should be secured in place during travel to prevent shifting. Many RV owners build custom mounts or use heavy-duty straps. Lighter units (under 40 pounds) are easier to move but may still need securing on rough roads.

Final Thoughts

A portable power station transforms the RV experience. No more hunting for hookups, no more generator noise at 6 AM, and no more worrying about whether your food will stay cold. The right unit depends on how you use your RV: full-timers should look at the EcoFlow Delta Pro 3, value-seekers at the Bluetti AC200L, and weekenders at the Jackery Explorer 2000 v2.
